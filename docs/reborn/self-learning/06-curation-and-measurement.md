# 6 — Keep It Healthy and Honest

Status: engineering design, proposed. Sixth and final increment of the [self-learning design](00-overview.md). The previous increments produce learned facts, rules, and skills; this one keeps the accumulating pile from rotting, proves it's actually helping rather than hurting, and gives humans the controls to see and steer it. It comes last not because it's optional but because it has the most to work on once everything else exists — and because un-curated memory is known to make agents *worse*, this is the increment that keeps the whole effort earning its place.

This increment is large enough that it's honestly a stack of four PRs, and they land in dependency order: the scheduled curation pass first (it only needs the lifecycle and the ledger from increment 2), then canary-driven demotion (it needs the rate-based failure signal from increments 4 and 5), then the measurement harness (it reads the curated store the first two produce), and finally the control surface (it surfaces everything the first three create). They're described together because they share one substrate and one set of invariants, but each ships and earns its keep on its own, and a reader should feel free to think of measurement and controls as their own deliverables that happen to be documented here.

## The problem this slice solves

Three problems show up only after the system has been learning for a while. First, accumulation: duplicates pile up, new facts contradict old ones, and stale items linger — and this isn't hypothetical, it's measured. On long-term-memory benchmarks like LOCOMO and LongMemEval, a naive, accumulating memory layer loses to simply pasting the raw history into the context window, and over-compressing memories into terse facts measurably hurts. The lesson isn't "memory is bad," it's "*un-curated* memory is bad," which makes curation the thing that justifies having memory at all. Second, the honesty problem: without a measurement, nobody actually knows whether the learning loop is helping or quietly degrading the agent. Third, the trust problem: humans need to see what's been learned, understand why, and be able to pause or override it — without that being the only safety mechanism.

## Curation: keeping the pile healthy

A scheduled background pass walks the active and candidate artifacts and keeps them healthy with very little reliance on a model — the one deliberate exception is contradiction detection, which is genuinely a comprehension problem and is handled the way every model call in this design is handled, with the model only *proposing* and a deterministic step disposing. Everything else is mechanical.

The single most important property of this pass is the one word the rest of the increment leans on: **scoped**. It is not an adjective here, it's an invariant. Every operation that compares two items and keeps one — merge, contradiction-supersession, demotion, budget eviction — partitions the artifacts by scope and owner first and acts strictly *within* a single partition. A narrow-scope item may never supersede, demote, or evict a broader-scope or different-owner item; the comparison is simply never set up across that line. This matters because supersession and demotion are the one place curation *removes* something from retrieval, and an unscoped removal is a cross-tenant leak waiting to happen — one user's item quietly knocking out another's. So any contradiction that spans scopes or owners is not resolved by the pass at all; it is routed to human review, because the machine has no safe basis for picking a winner across a trust boundary.

Within a scope, the pass does five things.

It **merges duplicates**, matching on content hash and high similarity with the same polarity, so it never collapses "X is true" into "X is false." On a merge it keeps the earliest item's identity and its provenance lineage — the surviving item still points back at every source it was learned from — but it refreshes recency and expiry from the *latest* occurrence, because increment 2's freshness model decays an item by how recently it was touched, and an item that just recurred is not stale just because its first sighting was old. The audience of the survivor is the *intersection* of the merged items' audiences, never the union: a merge can only ever narrow who is allowed to see the result, because widening it would let a duplicate seen in a broad conversation quietly expand the reach of one captured in a private one. And a merge **archives** the absorbed items rather than hard-deleting them, the same never-delete discipline the whole store runs under.

On a **contradiction** it doesn't silently overwrite the old item, and — this is the part that takes care — it doesn't silently demote the old one either. Detecting that two items contradict is comprehension, so a constrained model *proposes* the contradiction; a deterministic step then disposes. The disposition is deliberately conservative: a proposed supersession does not reach in and demote an active item, because doing that on a false positive would actively suppress a true fact with no gate, which is exactly the failure increments 2 and 4 built the candidate lifecycle to prevent. Instead the proposed replacement enters as a *candidate* superseding the old item, and the pair is routed to review (or, where the contradiction is between an active item and a clear duplicate of the same polarity, handled as a merge instead). The active item stays active and surfacing until something with authority — verification or a human — actually decides. Only then is the old item linked as superseded and demoted out of retrieval, with the history preserved. Versioned supersession is the established safe pattern, and Zep is the clean exemplar: it invalidates a fact by stamping the interval over which it was true rather than deleting it, so the superseded version is still there to read. (mem0 is sometimes cited alongside Zep here, but it isn't the same thing: base mem0's UPDATE overwrites the fact in place, and the invalidate-rather-than-delete behavior exists only in its graph variant — and even there the retained record is an audit log of the change, not a queryable history of superseded versions. So Zep, not mem0, is the pattern we're following.) Silent overwrite is the thing to avoid, because it destroys the ability to tell what changed and to roll back.

It **ages stale items into the archive** rather than deleting them, consistent with the standing rule that learned data is hidden and aged out, never destroyed.

It **demotes** a rule or skill whose live failure rate has crossed the same rate-based threshold that increments 4 and 5 use — a sustained failure rate or N consecutive failures, not a single returned failure, because a single failure under a flaky dependency would otherwise thrash a good rule in and out of retirement. Infrastructure and timeout error categories are discounted from that rate, since a network blip is not the rule being wrong, and there's a debounce so a brief spike doesn't trip it. Demotion here is a *transition* — active back to candidate for repair, the same transition increments 4 and 5 define — not a separate state, and the skill case also covers the drift re-check from increment 5: a skill whose dependencies have moved is re-run against its contract and demoted if it no longer passes.

And it **holds each slot to a budget**, archiving items beyond it so no category can grow without bound — the budget is the forcing function that turns "remember everything" into "remember the few things worth the space." But *which* items get archived can't be "the lowest-ranked," because increment 1's comparator is query-relative — an item's rank only exists relative to a specific moment's query, and there's no query here. Falling back to "least-used" would be worse: it's the closed-loop usage optimizer this design explicitly forbids, and it would evict the rare-but-critical guardrail — the one that almost never fires precisely because it's working — before any chatty, frequently-matched note. So budget eviction uses a query-independent, risk-aware order instead. An active high-risk guardrail is never evicted by the budget at all; it leaves only on real failure, expiry, or revocation. The rest are ordered by staleness and decay first, then by how reliable their source is, with usage allowed in only as a weak final tiebreaker between two items that are otherwise indistinguishable — never as the primary key.

One restraint runs under all of this: usage counts nudge how salient an item is, but they are never allowed to become a closed-loop optimizer that ships changes because some proxy number moved. That direction leads to reward-hacking — a system optimizing a metric into nonsense — and it's an explicit non-goal. Usage informs ranking and breaks ties; it never promotes, and it never, on its own, evicts.

Because this pass mutates the same artifacts the live system is reading and writing, it runs with a concurrency discipline rather than assuming it has the store to itself. Usage and recurrence counts are folded in with atomic increments, never read-modify-write. A supersession or merge runs in a transaction that re-checks, at commit time, that the loser hasn't been refreshed or re-activated since the pass selected it — if it has, the operation is abandoned and the pair is re-evaluated on the next sweep rather than acting on a stale view. And the per-(owner, source) idempotency that increments 2 and 4 already use carries through here, so a sweep that overlaps a live write doesn't double-count or clobber it.

## Measurement: is any of this actually helping?

The honest way to ask "did the harness learn" is an **ablation**, not a self-report. Take a task — ideally one drawn from a real recurring failure, so the loop is tested on the very mistakes it claims to fix — and run it three ways with the *same underlying model held fixed*: with no carried memory, with the raw prior material made available uncurated, and with the curated learned context. The curated version has to beat *both* of the others. Beating the no-memory baseline is the obvious bar; beating the dump-everything baseline is the one people skip, and it's the one that actually proves curation is earning its keep, because if curated memory can't beat just pasting in the raw history then the curation isn't doing anything.

Two things make this measurable that a naive version would get wrong. The first is the backward-transfer half — does new learning quietly *break* old behavior, which is the alarm for catastrophic forgetting. Memory is being continuously curated, so you can't just re-run an old task against "the memory" and compare, because the memory has moved since then. Backward transfer has to be measured against *point-in-time snapshots* of the store: freeze the state at the moment a task last passed, freeze it again now, and compare the same task across the two snapshots. Without the snapshot the question is unanswerable, because there's no stable "before" to regress against. The second is the "raw prior material" baseline: it has to respect the *same per-scope permission filter* as the curated arm. If the curated arm only sees what the running owner is permitted to see but the raw-dump baseline gets the unfiltered history, the eval is leaking one user's material into another user's task — and a memory system whose whole point is permission-scoped recall must not be measured by a harness that ignores the scope. Both arms read through the same scope and audience gate the live planner does.

Holding the base model fixed is what makes the gain number meaningful. The benchmark literature is candid that a with-versus-without-memory delta doesn't cleanly separate "the harness got smarter" from "the underlying model is just better," because a stronger model also uses retrieved memory better — so the only way to isolate the harness is to vary the memory while holding the model constant, and to read the result as an ablation rather than a single headline score. Alongside the gain, the two transfer questions are tracked together: does new learning help *later* tasks (forward transfer), and does it break *old* ones (backward transfer).

The top recurring failures are also re-run live now and then as standing canaries, to catch a regression that slips back in silently — but a canary that re-runs a high-risk task is a real hazard, because high-risk in this design *means* external writes, and re-running "send the reconnect email" could send the email again, for real, with the user's credentials. So canaries are bounded on every axis. They run against recorded fixtures or a no-side-effect sandbox, never against live external systems with the user's credentials; any task whose failure can't be reproduced without a real side effect is simply excluded from the canary set rather than run carefully. They run on a bounded cadence and within a budget, the same background plane and rationing the rule and skill verifiers use. A standing canary is a regression check, not a way to quietly re-execute the user's irreversible actions on a timer.

One discipline keeps the measurement honest: where a task's success can't be checked mechanically, a model may be used to *grade* the open-ended output — but only as a grader for the measurement, never as the gate that promotes a learned item. Those are different jobs, and conflating them is exactly how a model's opinion gets laundered into an acceptance decision, which the whole verification story (increment 4) exists to prevent.

## Controls: visibility and override, without being the safety model

Humans get real controls, and the design principle is that they're a *backstop and a steering wheel*, not the thing standing between every learned item and disaster — automatic verification already does that, which is what lets routine learning happen without a human in every loop. Through the product surface, a user or admin can pause learning entirely; pause the *use* of what's been learned independently of pausing new learning; inspect items in any state; approve or reject the high-risk ones that policy routed to review; suppress or archive items; see an item's sources and the evidence behind it; ask why a given memory or rule was used or skipped on a particular request; and see when learning sent data to a model provider for classification or verification.

It's worth being precise about the states, because two of them are easy to get wrong. The inspectable states are the real lifecycle states — candidate, active, suppressed, expired, revoked, and *archived*, which is curation's single largest output and so has to be a first-class thing a human can browse, not a black hole items fall into. *Demoted* is deliberately not in that list: demotion is a transition (active back to candidate for repair, per increments 4 and 5), not a resting state an item sits in, and listing it as a peer state would misrepresent the lifecycle.

The two pauses also enforce in two different places, and both fail closed. Pausing the *use* of learned items is enforced at the single use-time check in the planner — the same place every scope and audience check already runs — so a pause takes hold on the very next turn and, if the flag can't be read, the planner withholds rather than surfaces. Pausing *learning* is enforced at the signal-to-background boundary: signals stop being turned into stored items, and if that flag is unreadable the pipeline declines to learn rather than learning blind. The catastrophic-forgetting alarm from the measurement half is wired through to these same controls as an actual response, not just a dashboard light: when backward transfer regresses, the implicated items are automatically demoted out of retrieval and the regression is raised to review, so the alarm *does* something on its own while a human looks.

That set — explaining why something was or wasn't used — is the payoff for two artifacts the earlier increments paid to build. Increment 1's planner records its omission reasons, and increment 2 keeps an append-only ledger that stamps every lifecycle transition with its reason and timestamp. By the time a human asks "why did the agent use that, or skip that," the answer is a read-back of the ledger and the omission record, not a forensic reconstruction — the trail was written at the moment of each decision, which is the only time it could have been written honestly. This increment introduces no new logging of its own; it *consumes* what increments 1 and 2 already record. And every one of these controls goes through the same permission-checked path as the agent's own actions; the UI never touches the raw stores directly, so a control surface can never become a way around the scope, source, and expiry rules the rest of the system works to enforce.

## What this completes

With this increment the loop is whole: the agent learns out of band, stores what it learns scoped and attributed and expiring, verifies anything that changes behavior, keeps the result curated so it doesn't rot, measures whether it's actually helping, and shows its work to the humans who own it. Each piece shipped on its own and delivered value on its own, but together they're the thing the overview promised — an agent that reliably doesn't make the same mistake twice, and that can prove it.

## Open questions for this increment

The curation cadence, the debounce on rate-based demotion, and the per-slot budgets are tuning. The staleness-and-decay-versus-source-reliability weighting in budget eviction needs real values too, since it decides what survives when a slot is full. The standing evaluation task set — which real failures become permanent regression checks, and which of them can be reproduced safely against fixtures versus excluded as too side-effectful to canary — needs to be built and maintained, and it's the part most likely to be under-invested in. The similarity threshold for "this is a duplicate" and the contradiction-proposal threshold need real values, since both directly shape what survives, and the contradiction model's precision sets how much human-review load the cross-scope-and-active-conflict routing generates. And the review-queue ergonomics — how a human is shown a high-risk candidate and its evidence so they can decide quickly — are a product problem that determines whether the human controls actually get used.
